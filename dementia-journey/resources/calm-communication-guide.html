The Calm Communication Guide

One page, whole family. These work best when everyone who helps with care uses the same approach. Post it where you'll all see it.

Before words · the Positive Physical Approach™How to arrive

From the front, in their line of sight · slow to half speed, pause an arm's length away · greet with an open hand, a small wave · say their name first ("June."), then yours if needed · offer your hand and let them take it · come alongside, not head-on · get to eye level · connect warmly before the ask.

From Teepa Snow's Positive Approach to Care®, used under license. Our own caregivers train in this.

WordsOne thought at a time

If a sentence has an "and" in it, make it two sentences with a breath between. Say the noun, skip the pronoun ("put on the blue coat," not "put it on"). Hold up the object as you name it.

PaceThe ten-second pause

After one sentence, wait ten real seconds. Count them. Rephrasing too soon hands them a brand-new sentence to start over on. Silence isn't failure; silence is processing.

ChoicesTwo doors, both good

"Blue blouse or the yellow?" · "Tea or coffee?" · "Shower now or after breakfast?" Show both when you can. On hard days, one warm suggestion: "The blue one's your favorite. Here we go."

HelpingThe cueing ladder

  • 1. Words: "Your comb is on the sink."
  • 2. Show: point, or place it in view.
  • 3. Demonstrate: mime it; mirroring outlives language.
  • 4. Guide gently: help the motion start, ease off as they take over.
  • 5. Do only the part they can't. Finishing something feels like being someone.

AlwaysThe dignity list · retired habits

No talking about them as if they're not there · no public corrections · no baby talk · no quizzing · never "don't you remember?" · no discussing private care in front of visitors.
